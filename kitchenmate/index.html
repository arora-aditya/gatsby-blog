« KitchenMate - an intern's story

December 23, 2019 • ☕️☕️ 8 min read

UniversityCoopReflection

I am writing this blog as I head home having completed my third coop term [internship] at KitchenMate. By the time you read this, some time may have passed since I wrote this, mostly because I do not want to breach my confidentiality clause, and need to double check with the folks at KitchenMate.

When I actually got offered the job, it had been the first term in which I had not found a job in the main round process and was deferred to the continuous round during which I was able to find my third coop as a Software Engineer at KitchenMate. [context: most big companies finish their hiring in the main round]

I definitely had my qualms about the role, the first being that the interview had started on a delay in a bit of haphazard manner despite me being the first interviewee of the day. Secondly the fact that as an international student paying an insane amount of tuition, I could not accept a lowball salary in a city as expensive as Toronto [which a lot of companies were doing in that round]. All of my qualms were cluttering my head, while my interviewers raised concerns about me not being eligible for certain government grants.

Through a turn of events, at the end of the interview that day, I was confident that my previous experience, albeit split between Full Stack Development and Data Engineering had met its match in what the interviewees were looking for. As for the salary, I got offered an hourly pay lesser than my first and second coop, but the offer was justifiably better than my other offers, and I was fairly desperate to not have to go through another round of applying and interviewing with a new set of companies. [side note: both my first two salaries were some considered to be excessive by some for my experience]

On September 9, when I first joined the company, I was a little taken aback by the software practices, and the tech-stack having never worked with a similar workflow before, but as time went on me along with my co-worker (fellow coop and roommate, Rock Zhou) managed to reason our way towards improvements in the software processes. The changes were for the better, and the results were observable in the weeks following those discussions. By the time that I was ending my internship, all my initial qualms and concerns had been long quashed.

During the four months at KitchenMate, I was the everything engineer and did pretty much anything that relied on writing a line of code. Anything from writing complicated input driven SQL queries that joined on multiple tables, to add a feature to our mobile app and website, to writing embedded systems code for the proprietary smart cooker. Altogether I felt that the work that I was doing was very high impact to the overall product, and was definitely going to live on after I ended my coop.

During my time at KitchenMate, in a team of about 20 people, I had worked directly with 17 of them not including myself, and had tried to make each of their daily processes more automated and data driven. Before me, the team had access to a limited number of metrics and any requests that came in, always were deferred due to the lack of a dedicated data person. When I joined, since there was a severe backlog of these requests, having had past experience with data I took it upon myself to start delivering usable SQL dashboards that would control and drive the daily decision making process for different parts of the team. It took a while, but by the end of it I was referred to as “the data [science] guy” and was told that ”[I] probably knew the most about the data itself.” Realizing the fact that I would be filling the role of data scientist on the team for a sheer period of 3.5 months, I concluded that I would have to offset some of the responsibilities before I left so as to not leave a gaping hole. Over the last month or so, I tried to spend some time with some key members of the team, that had simple data requests that they could easily satisfy themselves with a little help from the tool that we were already using. After spending about an hour with each of these people, they felt empowered and enlightened about the powers of our data, and how it had already reduced their workload.

As for the embedded systems part, I had made it a personal goal to learn more embedded systems programming after taking a systems programming course the previous term, and coding for the smart cooker provided that perfect opportunity. I was able to ramp up fairly quickly to the new codebase, and went on to refactor some of the old code as well to improve the overall structure of our new release. The biggest part of my embedded systems tasks, was actually the user design phase where-in I actually had to apply some of what I learnt in my entrepreneurship course to conduct user experience interviews on the design choices that the engineering team had made to predict the impact on overall usability, positive or negative.

As a whole, the company is trying to solve a very difficult problem of food, with very innovative solution, and during this process there are some very hard problems that arise that are shaping up to be the core of the product offering, and it was those problems, those almost-unsolvable ones that drove me to put in my best.

Everyday that I went to work, I was excited by what new I was learning in embedded systems, while still being in touch with my strengths in Full Stack Development and Data Engineering. Couple this with three great team socials, and an overall relatively low-stress work environment allows this coop to easily take the lead for my favorite coop yet.
